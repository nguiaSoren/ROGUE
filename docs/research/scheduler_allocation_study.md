# Allocation as a Capability-Growth Mechanism: Telemetry-Driven Scheduling in a Self-Growing LLM Red-Team

*Working analysis, 2026-06-03. Local/WIP. Source telemetry: `ladder_attempts`, `ladder_rotation_membership`, `breach_results` on the live Neon instance; runs `sweep_p2_1780457963` (greedy baseline) and `sweep_starv_q3_1780462736` (causal test). Companion lab notes: `adaptive_orchestration_systems.md`.*

## Abstract

A continuous open-web LLM red-team grows its attack repertoire by harvesting reusable *techniques* (not just payloads) and graduating those that prove effective when reproduced against customer deployment configurations. We report that the binding constraint on repertoire growth was not technique quality but evaluation **allocation**. A greedy, cost-optimal escalation scheduler — which front-loads the historically best-performing transform and short-circuits on the first breach — starved high-value harvested candidates by never reaching them, so they appeared worthless. The failure was invisible because the system logged only *outcomes* (what ran), not *opportunities* (what could have run). After instrumenting reachability so that "evaluated and failed" is distinguishable from "never evaluated," we measured that 85% of all eligible strategy-appearances were lost to early-stop, with the candidate-bearing tier reaching only 7% reachability. Adding starvation-aware ordering and an evaluation quota drove that tier from 7% to 98% reachability and overall starvation from 85% to 1%; in controlled paid experiments, making the starved candidates reachable converted them from apparently-worthless to graduating — first an existence proof (3 of 3 admitted candidates graduated), then, on a deliberately least-favourable sample of the candidates the greedy scheduler had never reached, **8 of 20 (40%) graduated versus 0 of 20 under the identical-input greedy baseline** (Fisher exact *p* = 0.0033). We report the N=20 result as the headline (a bottleneck removed, not a smooth capability curve) and the 3/3 as the existence proof it is. The contribution is less a scheduling algorithm than a reframing: in a self-growing system, evaluation allocation is not an efficiency layer but a **capability-growth mechanism**, and the bottleneck it exposes next is candidate-selection policy, not strategy ordering.

## 1. The problem: orchestration, not cleverness, gates growth

Most red-team tooling collects payloads — specific prompt strings. This system instead harvests *techniques*: reusable methods ("escalate across turns," "split the intent then recompose," "render the objective as an image") that apply to many objectives, so harvesting compounds. A harvested technique enters as a `candidate` and graduates to `active` only when it wins a real reproduction. The intuition that growth is gated by *which techniques we can find and how good they are* turns out to be wrong. Growth is gated by **orchestration**: whether a harvested technique is ever actually evaluated, and how a fixed budget of evaluations is allocated across competing strategies. This study traces how a cost-optimal scheduler silently throttled growth, how the right telemetry made that visible, and how a small allocation change converted starved candidates into graduated capabilities.

## 2. System under study

Reproduction of a resisted ("EVADE-band") attack runs an **escalation ladder**: an ordered sequence of ~18 strategies across five tiers — image renderers, chain-of-jailbreak edits, structured-data injection, audio styles, and a planner-driven multi-turn tier that includes harvested candidates — tried in order against a panel of target models, short-circuiting on the first breach. Graduation feeds back from the ladder outcome: any harvested strategy that itself breaches is graduated (`apply_ladder_outcome` → `record_trial` → `graduate`). Two facts about this design are load-bearing. First, the ladder runs one global ordering against a *multi-model* panel and stops at the first breach, so it optimizes "reach a breach cheaply," not "evaluate every candidate." Second, because it stops early, the planner tier — which sits last and carries the candidates — frequently never runs at all.

## 3. Methodological core: measuring opportunity, not just outcome

The central methodological move is separating two kinds of failure that a naive log conflates. A strategy can fail because it was *evaluated and the attack didn't work* (capability failure) or because it was *never evaluated* (orchestration failure: a planner refusal, a render error, or — most importantly — early-stop reaching a breach before this strategy ran). Two instruments make this separable.

**The valid-trial split.** Every ladder attempt is typed (`breach` / `no_breach` / `refused` / `render_error`) and the lifecycle tracks `n_attempts_total` (orchestration reach) separately from `n_valid_trials` (real semantic tests: breach or no_breach only). Retirement and value scoring then measure attack failure, not orchestration health — a strategy is not penalized for being refused, only for being tested and losing.

**Reachability telemetry.** The deeper instrument logs the *full eligible rotation* per ladder, not just what executed. Before it, a missing row was ambiguous across four distinct realities: never eligible (e.g., audio with no audio-capable config), starved by early-stop, ranked too low to be reached, or cut off by budget. The `ladder_rotation_membership` table records, for every eligible strategy in every ladder, its rank, whether it was eligible, whether it executed, and — if not — the reason (`early_stop` / `budget` / `no_compatible_config` / `not_reached`). This is reconstructed post-hoc from the ladder result, so the working, money-spending execution path is never touched. With it, reachability (`executed ÷ eligible`) and starvation rate (`early_stopped ÷ eligible`) become first-class, queryable signals. The conceptual shift is from a system that knows *outcomes* to one that knows *opportunities* — it can finally answer "what could have happened but didn't," which is the information most orchestration systems never collect.

## 4. Experimental sequence

The work proceeded as a sequence of small, individually-verifiable increments, each motivated by the telemetry rather than designed top-down.

- **Phase 1 — fixed ordering.** The ladder tried strategies in a hand-coded order. Telemetry surfaced renderer dominance: image-tier transforms absorbed nearly every breach, and harvested candidates showed zero trials.
- **Phase 2 — greedy reorder.** Each tier's strategies were sorted by Laplace-smoothed historical breach rate (unseen strategies get a 0.5 prior so they sort ahead of proven-weak incumbents — cold-start survivability), changing evaluation priority only, leaving execution untouched. Modes: `canonical` (deterministic argmax), `discovery` (UCB-style optimism), `fixed` (legacy).
- **Phase 2.1 — reachability telemetry.** The instrument of §3, deployed so the cost of greedy ordering could be measured rather than assumed.
- **Phase 2.2 — starvation-aware ordering + quota.** Value is scored as effectiveness × viability × freshness × exploration, then adjusted by `× (1 + W · starvation_rate)` — reachability as *exploration pressure on the starved*, deliberately **not** `value × reachability` (which would reinforce incumbents: high reach → more wins → higher value → more reach). The complementary cross-tier lever is `candidate_quota`, which suppresses early-stop until N candidates have been attempted.

A zero-cost simulation preceded the second paid run: because the quota mechanic is deterministic given the rotation, the logged rotation-membership rows were replayed to estimate reachability and cost under quota 0/1/2/3. Its decisive output is that the cost of suppressing early-stop is binary at quota 0→1 (\$2.80 → \$18.45, ≈6×, because reaching even one starved candidate runs almost the whole ladder), with quota 1→3 adding nothing further, and that planner-tier reachability jumps from 0.07 toward full once the cap lifts. The operational rule "if you pay the suppression cost at all, go to the full quota" was thus established for free, with no cheap partial-quota sweet spot. (The replay bounds reachability and cost; whether a now-reachable candidate *breaches* needs the paid run, which we then ran.)

## 5. Results

### 5.1 The greedy tradeoff, measured

The greedy reorder is a *correct* optimizer of its stated objective. On a 40-primitive paid sweep (`canonical`, `quota=0`), the winner sat at rotation rank 0 in the median ladder — the likely winner was tried first — and escalation cost \$0.35 per breach. But the reachability instrument showed the price: **85% of all eligible strategy-appearances were lost to early-stop**, with per-tier reachability of 0.40 (image), 0.17 (coj), 0.12 (structured/audio), and **0.07 (planner)**. Six strategies were high-value yet low-reachability — including three candidates at value ≈ 0.50, reachability 0.00, starvation 1.00. These were not weak candidates; they were *invisible* ones, and the run produced **zero graduations**.

### 5.2 Allocation bias: winner attribution is not vulnerability

A secondary, generalizable finding emerged from comparing the ladder's per-model winner distribution against the unbiased per-model breach matrix from full reproduction (`breach_results`, 10,872 trials across the six-model panel). Because the ladder short-circuits at the first breaching model in panel order, its winner attribution is nearly *inverted* from true vulnerability: GPT-class won 62% of ladders while breaching only 11% in the full matrix (+51 points), whereas the most-vulnerable model (49% unbiased) won just 12% (−37 points). The lesson is methodological: short-circuit winner counts cannot support per-model conclusions; only the full matrix can. This also bounds what contextual `(strategy × model)` priors can be learned from the ladder — they cannot, which is why contextual priors in this system are sourced from the unbiased matrix and used as warm-prior/analysis rather than as a live reorder driver. (A separate, *ordering*-scoped result — that cross-tier reorder is itself a capability lever, converting depth-cap-unreachable winners into breaches — is out of this study's admission/budget scope; see `adaptive_orchestration_paper.md` §6b and the ATP sections of `adaptive_orchestration_systems.md`.)

### 5.3 The causal test

The single unresolved question after Phase 2.2 was causal: *if candidates become reachable, do they breach and graduate?* No amount of simulation, telemetry, or reordering could answer it — only running the candidates could. The controlled experiment held the primitive set fixed at the same 40 and changed only the variables under test: `starvation` ordering + `candidate_quota=3` + `n_trials=1`, escalation capped at \$25.

| Metric | Greedy baseline (quota=0) | Starvation + quota=3 |
|---|---|---|
| Overall starvation | 85% | **1%** |
| Planner-tier reachability | 0.07 | **0.98** |
| Candidate reachability | 0.00 | **~1.00** |
| Candidates breached (of those selected) | 0 (never ran) | **3 of 3** |
| Graduations | **0** | **3** (active 7 → 10) |
| Winner rank (median) | 0 | 1 |
| Escalation cost / breach | \$0.35 | \$2.51 |

The answer is unambiguous. Every candidate that the selection policy admitted to the rotation breached once it was actually evaluated, and every one of those graduated — a +43% jump in active repertoire in a single run, against zero under the identical-input greedy baseline. The starvation-aware ordering additionally de-monopolized the image tier without penalizing the strong incumbent: the previously-starved `mml:base64` rose to win four ladders alongside `mml:wr`'s four, where the baseline had `mml:wr` winning six of eight.

### 5.3b The rate, at N=20 (2026-06-13)

Three-of-three is an existence proof, and a small one: with n=3 the graduation rate is indistinguishable from anything between 30% and 100%, and a single non-breach would have halved it. It also flattered the candidates — those three, and the four that graduated in the K=5 follow-up, were among the most-tried in the pool. To get a rate rather than an anecdote we raised the candidate-selection cap to 25 and the quota to 20 (`CAND_LADDER_CAP=25`, `--single-quota 20`), which let a single sweep evaluate the *least-tried* fresh candidates — the population the greedy scheduler had never touched — against the four EVADE parents that reached the planner tier (run `abq_1781308343`, all-in cost \$14.57).

Twenty distinct candidates were evaluated; **eight breached and graduated (40%)**, against the zero the greedy scheduler evaluates of that same set. Fisher's exact test on 8/20 versus 0/20 gives a two-sided p = 0.0033 (one-sided 0.0016). Two things are worth stating plainly. First, the honest rate is **40%, not the 87.5% the small-N sweeps suggested** — fresh, least-tried candidates breach far less often than the head of the pool, exactly as a survivorship account predicts, and the earlier number should be read as the optimistic tail it was. Second, the contrast is not a fresh randomized arm on these exact twenty: the "0/20" is the established greedy-starvation result (planner-tier reachability ~7%), and the clean matched comparison remains the original q0-vs-q3 arm above. What the larger N buys is not a better p-value — 3/3 was already enough to reject chance — but a defensible *rate*: reaching the starved tail of the pool still converts roughly two in five otherwise-invisible candidates into permanent capability.

The cost moved the *right* way. This sweep graduated eight techniques for ≈\$11.50 of escalation, **\$1.44 per graduation** — below the \$7.01 at K=5 and \$8.37 at K=3 (Figure F4). The economic inversion of §6 therefore holds out to K=20: yield per candidate falls as you reach deeper into the pool, but yield per dollar keeps rising, because the fixed full-rotation cost is amortized over more graduations. The saturation point — where cost-per-graduation finally turns up — is past K=20 and still unmeasured.

### 5.4 Interpretation

The scheduler was never *filtering* bad candidates; it was *preventing their evaluation*. This is the result's weight: in a self-growing system, the allocation policy does not merely make a fixed capability cheaper to apply — it determines whether latent capability is ever realized. Allocation is a capability-growth mechanism. The cost is the honest counterweight: quota=3 runs the full rotation, so escalation cost-per-breach rose ~7× (\$0.35 → \$2.51). The correct operational reading is therefore not "run quota=3 always" but "run it deliberately as a periodic *growth sweep*," leaving the cheap greedy mode as the default for routine reproduction.

## 6. A correction, recorded honestly

During analysis we initially claimed a "winner-only graduation undercount" — that nine candidates breached but only three graduated, leaving six uncredited. Verification before any code refuted this. Graduation is gated on `won`, and `apply_ladder_outcome` sets `won = (outcome == "breach")` for *every* harvested strategy in the attempt list, so any breaching candidate already graduates; "winner-only" is merely *emergent* in early-stop mode, where only the winner runs. The nine breaching techniques were three candidates (all graduated) plus six *already-active* techniques (which cannot re-graduate). There was no undercount, and no fix was built. The attribution rule is already mode-adaptive by construction. We record this not as an aside but because the discipline that mattered throughout — distrusting a plausible interpretation until the telemetry confirms it — applies equally to one's own analysis, and it averted a needless feature.

## 7. Limitations and honesty ledger

- **Statistical power.** An earlier grammar-efficacy A/B was variance-dominated: arms swapped order across runs at N≈16/arm, so per-arm breach differences were run-to-run noise, not the arm. Single paid sweeps are directional, not significance-tested; the causal result rests on a categorical change (0 → 3 graduations, 0.07 → 0.98 reachability) large relative to plausible noise, not on a tight interval.
- **Within-tier vs cross-tier.** Starvation-aware ordering operates within tiers; it breaks the image-tier monopoly but cannot move candidates across tiers. The cross-tier reach in §5.3 came from the quota, not the ordering. The two are complements, and conflating them would misattribute the effect.
- **Cost realism.** The Mistral planner is priced at \$0 in the cost log, so planner spend is currently unaccounted; escalation cost-per-breach is measured from target+judge calls only. A second sweep was run unattended for hours before the per-request and wall-clock timeouts that now protect paid runs were in place.
- **The fixed primitive set.** Both sweeps used the same 40 primitives selected by reproducibility score; results may not extend to the full corpus, and `n_trials=1` in the causal run makes EVADE classification noisier than the `n_trials=5` baseline.

## 8. Selection budget is a dominant growth driver (Growth Sweep #2)

With reachability solved, the binding constraint relocated one step upstream to the selection policy, which admits only K candidates to the rotation per sweep. The pre-registered follow-up raised K from 3 to 5 (with quota raised in lockstep so all five were actually evaluated) and measured graduation yield and cost. The result was decisive and somewhat surprising: all five selected candidates were evaluated, four graduated (active 10 → 14, +40%), and combined across the two growth sweeps **seven of eight evaluated candidates graduated — 87.5% yield**. A junk pool would have collapsed on the second batch; it held at 4/5. The candidate population is not exhausted, and selection budget, not candidate quality, remains the binding constraint on growth.

The economic result is the more consequential one: **cost-per-graduation improved as K rose**, from $8.37 (K=3) to $7.01 (K=5). This inverts the usual exploration pattern of immediate diminishing returns, and it has a structural cause — under the evaluation quota each ladder runs the full rotation regardless, so harvested candidates ride along nearly for free. Ladder execution is a fixed cost; candidate evaluation is marginal. As long as candidates keep graduating, raising K amortizes the fixed full-rotation tax over more graduations and *improves* graduations-per-dollar. This reframes the optimization: candidate slots are not the scarce resource we had assumed.

The practical consequence is two explicit operating modes rather than one default. **Canonical mode** (K=3, quota=0, canonical ordering) optimizes for finding breaches cheaply — ~$0.35 per breach, median winner rank 0 — and remains the routine-reproduction default. **Growth mode** (K=quota, starvation ordering, K promoted 3→5 on this evidence; `scripts/growth_sweep.sh`) optimizes for repertoire growth at ~$7 per graduation, run deliberately. The two objectives are genuinely different and should not share a configuration. The remaining open question is the *saturation point*: a stopping rule of "raise K each growth sweep while cost-per-graduation stays flat or improves; stop when it rises sharply" is now well-defined and metric-driven. Current evidence (eight candidates evaluated, yield still high, cost-per-graduation still falling) justifies K=5 and a future probe toward K=8, but not maximizing K. Explicitly out of scope until the saturation point is mapped: Thompson sampling, contextual routing, hierarchical priors, and further reordering machinery — none address "how large should the evaluation budget be," which the evidence now identifies as the live lever.

## 9. Summary

The chain the work set out to establish resolved end to end: `reachable → tested → breach → graduate`. A cost-optimal scheduler had silently broken the first link, the right telemetry made the break measurable, and a small, deliberately-bounded allocation change repaired it — converting three invisible candidates into three graduated capabilities and a +43% repertoire increase, with a second growth sweep adding four more. The durable contribution is the reframing: evaluation allocation in a self-growing system is a lever on *capability*, not merely on *cost*, and the discipline of measuring opportunity rather than only outcome is what surfaced the next real constraint instead of optimizing prematurely against an understood one. The loop is finally closed by a deterministic growth scheduler that decides when to pay for growth from the candidate pool itself (`reproduce/growth_scheduler.py`), so the system does not merely demonstrate that it can self-expand but, given a harvest cadence, actually does — growth as system behaviour rather than a manual decision.

## Note — cross-reference to the Grammar-Component Predictive-Power Study (added 2026-06-06)

**The §7 underpowered authoring A/B is the direct motivation for the grammar-efficacy observational screen (#TRS-C), and the two studies are intended to operate in sequence.** The slot-fill A/B (`scripts/grammar_efficacy_ab.py`) left a large, unattributed gap — deterministic templates breached ~0.25 vs freeform model authoring ~0.44, both at 1.00 validity and zero orchestration failures — but it cannot answer whether that gap is a grammar-structure effect, a prompt-framing effect, a run-to-run variance artifact, or some combination. Before spending money on a powered causal test of that specific gap (#TRS-A, paired McNemar, N≥60/arm), the right cheap-first-pass question is: does grammar structure carry any predictive signal at all in the existing corpus? #TRS-C (observational, $0, `python scripts/grammar_study.py`) labels the existing primitives with `GrammarNode` structure and measures per-node lift with family-stratification + FDR controls over the ~1,540 per-(primitive × target) outcomes already in `breach_results`. If #TRS-C finds FDR-significant node or combination signal that survives family stratification — the signal criterion — then a powered #TRS-A run against those specific nodes is a well-targeted causal confirmation. If #TRS-C is null — no node clears the bar after controls — then #TRS-A loses most of its rationale (there is no grammar signal to causally test) and the §7 gap is more likely a prompt-framing or run-variance artifact, not a grammar-structure effect. In that scenario, engineering investment in AST elaboration and synthetic technique generation is deprioritized. The relationship is: **#TRS-C is the cheap observational screen; #TRS-A is the expensive causal confirmation; running them in order is the canonical cheap-screen-before-confirm pattern.** Full #TRS-C design: `docs/RESEARCH_TODO.md #TRS-C`. Methodology details and confound disclosures: `docs/adaptive_orchestration_systems.md §Grammar-Component Predictive-Power Study`.

## Note — cross-reference to the Technique Retrieval System (added 2026-06-06)

**The grammar-efficacy A/B in §7 is the upstream open question that retrieval does NOT close.** The slot-fill A/B (`scripts/grammar_efficacy_ab.py`) was underpowered: N≈16/arm, arms swapped order across runs, variance crossover, no measured breach lift. The large unattributed effect sitting underneath it — deterministic templates breached ~0.25 vs freeform model authoring ~0.44, both at 1.00 validity, zero orchestration failures — is real but unexplained. The bottleneck is statistical power in breach measurement, not generative expressiveness. The Technique Retrieval System (`scripts/retrieval_eval.py`; see `docs/adaptive_orchestration_systems.md` §Technique Retrieval Layer and `docs/RESEARCH_TODO.md #TRS-B`) addresses a different, downstream problem: it changes per-run cost from O(N-techniques) to O(K) by retrieving only the top-K most promising candidates for each target deployment. Retrieval makes large-scale technique corpus growth economically viable — but it scales the *input* to breach evaluation, not the signal-to-noise of breach evaluation itself. More candidates evaluated cheaply does not resolve whether structured composition predicts breach rate. The powered authoring-vs-template experiment (`docs/RESEARCH_TODO.md #TRS-A`, paired McNemar design, N≥60/arm, pre-registered stopping rule) remains the prerequisite before investing in grammar elaboration, AST composition, or synthetic technique generation — and retrieval does not substitute for it. Read the §7 statistical-power limitation in that light: it is not merely a methods caveat; it is the statement of what the next required experiment is.

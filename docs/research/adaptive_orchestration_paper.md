## Abstract

We study a continuous, open-web red-team for large language models that *grows its own attack repertoire*: it harvests reusable jailbreak and prompt-injection *techniques* from the open web, reproduces them against customer deployment configurations (model × system-prompt × tools), and *graduates* the ones that prove effective into a permanent repertoire. The central, somewhat counter-intuitive finding is that repertoire growth in such a system is gated far more by **orchestration** — which techniques are actually evaluated, and how a fixed evaluation budget is allocated — than by technique cleverness or quality. We report six results obtained over an instrumented, telemetry-first build. (1) *Provider willingness* to author attacks was the dominant early gate: an alignment-trained planner refused to author escalation plans for harvested directives, capping harvested-candidate validity at ~22%; changing **only** the planner backbone to a permissive model lifted it to 100% and graduated a technique the aligned planner had made *unreachable, not weak*. We then removed the dependence structurally by moving attack *structure* into deterministic, versioned grammars, demoting the model from author to parameter-filler. (2) A cost-optimal greedy escalation scheduler silently **starved** high-value harvested candidates — 85% of all eligible strategy-appearances never ran — a failure invisible until we instrumented *reachability*, distinguishing "evaluated and failed" from "never evaluated." (3) The ladder's short-circuit **winner attribution is nearly inverted** from true per-model vulnerability (up to +51 / −37 percentage points), so winner counts cannot support per-model conclusions; only the full reproduction matrix can. (4) In controlled paid experiments, making the starved candidates reachable converted them from apparently-worthless to graduating — first **3 of 3**, then, on a deliberately least-favourable sample of the *least-tried* candidates the greedy scheduler had never reached, **8 of 20 (40%)** versus **0** under the identical-input greedy baseline (Fisher exact p = 0.003), at a cost-per-graduation that *fell* to \$1.44. (5) The **evaluation budget K** (how many candidates are admitted per sweep) is a dominant and surprisingly *cheap* growth lever — cost-per-graduation *improved* as K rose ($8.37 → $7.01), because under an evaluation quota each ladder runs the full rotation regardless, making candidate evaluation a marginal cost on top of a fixed one. (6) A deterministic **growth scheduler** closes the loop, deciding when to pay for growth directly from candidate-pool size, turning self-expansion from a human decision into system behaviour. (7) In a clean single-variable experiment — repertoire, judge, corpus, and target held fixed, only strategy *order* changed — **cross-tier scheduling is itself a capability lever, not merely a cost optimization**: under a per-scan depth/budget cap the prior order exhausted the cap before reaching the winning strategy, leaving goals unbreached; re-ordering to reach that winner first **converts missed goals into breaches**, so attack-success rate, cost-per-breach, and latency all improve at once (ASR 30→45% and 50→60% across two datasets; cost-per-success −41 to −50%). The durable contribution is a reframing: in a self-growing system, evaluation *allocation* is a lever on *capability*, not merely cost, and the methodological discipline of measuring *opportunity* (what could have run) rather than only *outcome* (what ran) is precisely what surfaced the real constraints instead of optimizing prematurely against understood ones.

## 1. Introduction

Most red-team and jailbreak research measures **attack effectiveness**: a technique, a target, a success rate. The object of study here is different and, we argue, rarer — the **orchestration system** that decides, continuously and under a budget, *which* attacks to harvest, *whether* a harvested technique ever gets evaluated, and *how* a fixed evaluation budget is spread across competing strategies. The motivating system, ROGUE, is a self-growing red-team: it does not merely accumulate a static list of prompts but harvests reusable *methods* and promotes the effective ones into a repertoire it then reproduces daily against deployment configurations.

The intuition going in was that growth would be gated by the supply and quality of techniques — by harvesting and filtering. Repeatedly, and at every layer we instrumented, that intuition was wrong. Growth was gated by orchestration: by an aligned model's unwillingness to author attacks, by a cost-optimal scheduler that never evaluated the most promising candidates, and by an evaluation budget set too small. Each constraint was *invisible* in the system's logs until we changed *what* we logged — from outcomes to opportunities. The recurring methodological lesson, which we make explicit throughout, is that you can only schedule, retire, or grow on signals you can measure, and the highest-leverage instrumentation was the kind that records *what could have happened but did not*.

**Contributions.**
1. A characterization of *provider willingness* as a correctness-level gate on repertoire growth, and a structural removal of it via deterministic grammar orchestration (§3).
2. A telemetry methodology that separates *orchestration failure* from *capability failure* and makes *reachability* — opportunity, not outcome — a first-class, queryable signal (§4).
3. A measured account of how a cost-optimal greedy scheduler trades repertoire growth for efficiency, including a generalizable *allocation-bias* result showing that short-circuit winner attribution is unreliable for per-model conclusions (§5).
4. A controlled demonstration that *allocation*, not candidate quality, was the binding constraint — reachable candidates graduate — and that the *evaluation budget* is a dominant and economically inverted growth lever (§5–§6).
5. A single-variable controlled result that **scheduling is capability, not just optimization**: holding the repertoire, judge, corpus, and target fixed and changing *only* strategy order, cross-tier ordering converts depth-cap-unreachable winners into breaches — ASR↑, cost↓, and latency↓ *simultaneously*, consistent across two datasets (§6b).
6. A deterministic *growth scheduler* that closes the self-expansion loop, plus the operating modes the experiments revealed (§6–§7).
7. A reproducibility-and-operations account (deterministic grammars, per-request and wall-clock timeouts, a zero-cost simulation that pre-screened paid experiments) that we believe is a necessary, under-reported part of any honest adaptive-systems result (§8).

## 2. System and background

**Techniques, not payloads.** ROGUE's unit of growth is a reusable *technique* — "escalate across turns," "split the intent then recompose," "render the objective as an image" — rather than a specific prompt string. Techniques compound: one applies to many objectives. A harvested technique enters the lifecycle as a `candidate` (unproven) and is promoted to `active` only when it *wins a real reproduction*. The lifecycle also soft-retires never-winners and resurrects retired techniques on drift, so the repertoire self-prunes as well as self-grows.

**Reproduction and the escalation ladder.** To test whether a resisted ("EVADE-band") attack can be broken, ROGUE runs an *escalation ladder*: an ordered sequence of ~28 transform strategies across five tiers — image renderers, chain-of-jailbreak edits, structured-data injection, audio styles, and a planner-driven multi-turn tier that carries the harvested candidates — each tried in order against a panel of target models, **short-circuiting on the first breach**. The panel comprises six models spanning current cheap-tier offerings plus a frontier flagship and an older open-weight reliability anchor.

**Why this design starves candidates.** Two properties of the ladder are load-bearing throughout. First, it runs *one global ordering* against a *multi-model panel* and stops at the first breach, optimizing "reach a breach cheaply," not "evaluate every candidate." Second, because it stops early and the candidates sit *last* (the planner tier), a cheaper transform near the front almost always breaks some model first, and the ladder halts before the candidates ever run. The candidates are therefore the most likely strategies to be starved precisely because they are placed where the budget runs out.

## 3. Finding 1 — Provider willingness gates growth, and grammar orchestration removes it

**The planner as the binding gate.** A reproduction involves three model roles: a *judge* that grades outcomes, a *planner* that authors multi-turn escalation plans, and the *target* under test. Early telemetry showed the binding constraint sat in the planner role. With an alignment-trained planner (a Claude-class model), harvested candidates' *validity rate* — the fraction of attempts that were real semantic tests rather than orchestration failures — sat at roughly **22%**, dominated by the planner *refusing to author* escalation plans for harvested jailbreak directives. A controlled experiment changing **only** the planner backbone to a permissive model (`mistralai/mistral-small-2603`) took validity to **100%** and graduated a technique (internally "VERA") that the aligned planner had rendered *unreachable, not weak*. The architectural response was a separation of concerns that we adopt as a principle: a **safe judge + permissive planner + safe target** — the planner authors the attacks the defensive red-team must test against, while grading and the system under test stay aligned.

**From LLM-as-author to LLM-as-parameterizer.** A permissive default removed the immediate refusal, but it left growth hostage to *which provider refuses least this month* — an uncontrollable, drifting dependency. We removed the dependency structurally by moving the attack *structure* out of the model and into **deterministic, versioned grammars** (`StrategyTemplate`): an ordered turn-skeleton with typed slots, instantiated with no model call. The model, if used at all, fills *parameters* (semantic slot values), never *intent*. This demotes planner willingness from a *correctness* dependency (no plan ⇒ no test ⇒ no growth) to an *optimization* layer. The proof that the dependence is *removed* rather than *reduced*: with no API key and no network, the planner still returns a complete, valid multi-turn plan for a grammar-matched technique — the provider client is never even constructed (a property pinned by an offline unit test).

**The grammar-efficacy question, and an honest negative result.** A natural worry is that deterministic grammars preserve reliability at the cost of breach effectiveness. We ran a controlled A/B (deterministic templates vs. freeform model authoring) and report it including its negative: on the planner-driven tiers, templates breached at 0.25 and freeform at 0.44, *both* at 1.00 validity and 0 orchestration failures — but across repeated runs the two arms **swapped order** (templates 0.25↔0.44; freeform 0.44↔0.33). At N≈16 attempts per arm the per-arm breach-rate noise (standard error ≈ 0.12) exceeds the effect, so the experiment is **underpowered** and the apparent breach gap is dominated by run-to-run variance. We therefore could not establish a breach difference, and we shipped a *slot-fill* middle tier (model fills only the grammar's semantic slots, gated by a strict structural validator, with total fallback to the pure template) as a *reliability-neutral, structurally-safe* augmentation rather than a measured breach win. The relevant systems lesson is not the breach number but the variance: single paid sweeps in this regime are directional, not significance-tested, and we treat them accordingly for the remainder.

## 4. Methodological core — measuring orchestration, not just outcomes

The instrument that made every subsequent finding possible is the separation of two failure modes that a naive log conflates: a strategy can fail because it was *evaluated and the attack did not work* (capability failure) or because it was *never evaluated* (orchestration failure — a planner refusal, a render error, or, most importantly, early-stop reaching a breach before this strategy ran). Two pieces of substrate make the distinction measurable.

**The valid-trial split.** Every ladder attempt carries a typed outcome (`breach` / `no_breach` / `refused` / `render_error`), and the lifecycle tracks `n_attempts_total` (orchestration reach) separately from `n_valid_trials` (real semantic tests: breach or no_breach only). Retirement and value-scoring then measure *attack* failure, not orchestration health: a strategy is not retired for being refused, only for being tested and losing. The derived `validity_rate = valid_trials / attempts_total` becomes a queryable per-strategy signal — it is precisely how the planner-gating result of §3 was quantified.

**Reachability telemetry.** The deeper instrument logs the *full eligible rotation* per ladder, not only what executed. Before it, a *missing* attempt row was ambiguous across four distinct realities: never eligible (e.g., an audio strategy against a config with no audio capability), starved by early-stop, ranked too low to be reached, or cut off by budget. A dedicated append-only table records, for every eligible strategy in every ladder, its rank, whether it was eligible, whether it executed, and — if not — the reason (`early_stop` / `budget` / `no_compatible_config` / `not_reached`). The trace is reconstructed *post-hoc* from the ladder result, so the working, money-spending execution path is never modified. With it, `reachability = executed ÷ eligible` and `starvation_rate = early_stopped ÷ eligible` become first-class signals. We characterize the conceptual shift as moving from a system that knows *outcomes* (what ran) to one that knows *opportunities* (what could have run) — the latter is the information most orchestration systems never collect, and it is the precondition for honest allocation analysis.

## 5. Finding 2 — Allocation, not technique quality, gates growth

**The greedy tradeoff, measured.** A greedy reorder — sort each ladder tier by smoothed historical breach rate so the likely winner is tried first — is a *correct* optimizer of its stated objective. On a 40-primitive paid sweep (greedy ordering, no candidate quota) the winner sat at rotation rank 0 in the median ladder and escalation cost was **$0.35 per breach**. The reachability instrument exposed the price: **85% of all eligible strategy-appearances were lost to early-stop**, with per-tier reachability of 0.40 (image), 0.17 (chain-of-jailbreak), 0.12 (structured / audio), and **0.07 (planner)** — the candidate-bearing tier. Six strategies were high-value yet low-reachability, including three candidates at value ≈ 0.50, reachability 0.00, starvation 1.00. These were not weak candidates; they were *invisible* ones, and the run produced **zero graduations**. The greedy scheduler had not optimized growth into a cheaper form — it had optimized it away.

**Allocation bias: winner attribution is not vulnerability.** Comparing the ladder's per-model winner distribution against the *unbiased* per-model breach matrix from full reproduction (10,872 trials across the six-model panel) yields a generalizable result. Because the ladder short-circuits at the first breaching model in panel order, its winner attribution is nearly *inverted* from true vulnerability: a GPT-class model won 62% of ladders while breaching only 11% in the full matrix (+51 points), whereas the *most* vulnerable model (49% unbiased) won just 12% (−37 points). The methodological consequence: short-circuit winner counts cannot support per-model conclusions; only the full matrix can. This also bounds what *contextual* (per-model) priors can be learned from the ladder — they cannot, which is why our contextual effectiveness map is sourced from the unbiased reproduction matrix and used as an offline warm-prior rather than a live reorder driver.

**The causal test.** The unresolved question after instrumentation was strictly causal: *if a starved candidate is made reachable, does it breach and graduate?* No telemetry, simulation, or reordering could answer it — only running the candidates could. We held the primitive set fixed and changed only the variables under test: starvation-aware ordering plus a candidate quota of 3 (the quota suppresses early-stop until 3 candidates have been *attempted*, forcing the ladder past the first breach into the candidate tier).

| Metric | Greedy baseline (quota=0) | Starvation + quota=3 |
|---|---|---|
| Overall starvation | 85% | **1%** |
| Planner-tier reachability | 0.07 | **0.98** |
| Candidate reachability | 0.00 | **~1.00** |
| Candidates breached (of those selected) | 0 (never ran) | **3 of 3** |
| Graduations | **0** | **3** (active 7 → 10) |
| Winner rank (median) | 0 | 1 |
| Escalation cost / breach | $0.35 | $2.51 |

Every candidate admitted to the rotation breached once actually evaluated, and every one graduated — a +43% repertoire increase in a single run, against zero under the identical-input greedy baseline. The interpretation is the result's weight: **the scheduler was never filtering bad candidates; it was preventing their evaluation. Allocation is a capability-growth mechanism, not merely a cost optimization.**

Three is an existence proof, not a rate. A later sweep settled what the rate actually is. We raised the selection cap to 25 and the quota to 20 so one run would evaluate twenty *fresh, least-tried* candidates — precisely the tail of the pool the greedy scheduler never reaches — and ran it against the four EVADE parents that escalate to the planner tier (run `abq_1781308343`, all-in \$14.57). Of the twenty, **eight breached and graduated (40%)** against the zero greedy evaluates of that set; Fisher's exact on 8/20 vs 0/20 gives two-sided p = 0.003. The rate is the honest correction here: the head-of-pool sweeps suggested ~87.5%, but the least-tried tail graduates at *two in five*, which is what a survivorship account predicts and what a deployment should plan around. The "0/20" is the established greedy-starvation baseline, not a fresh randomized arm — the clean matched contrast remains the q0-vs-q3 experiment above; the larger N buys a defensible rate, not a smaller p. Cost moved the right way regardless: \$1.44 per graduation, below both prior sweeps (Figure F4).

**A correction, recorded.** During analysis we initially believed the lifecycle under-credited candidates ("winner-only graduation undercount"). Verification before any code refuted it: graduation is gated on `won`, and the outcome handler sets `won = (outcome == "breach")` for *every* harvested strategy in the attempt list, so any breaching candidate already graduates; "winner-only" is merely *emergent* in early-stop mode, where only the winner runs. The graduation rule is already mode-adaptive by construction, and no fix was warranted. We retain this not as an aside but because the discipline that mattered throughout — distrusting a plausible interpretation until the data confirm it — applies equally to one's own analysis and here averted a needless feature.

## 6. Finding 3 — Selection budget K is a dominant, cheap growth lever

With reachability solved, the binding constraint relocated upstream to the *selection* policy: of the candidate pool, only K candidates are admitted to the rotation per sweep (least-tried-first). K=3 yielded 3 graduations. A pre-registered follow-up raised K to 5 (with the quota raised in lockstep so all five were evaluated) and measured graduation yield and cost.

**Result.** All five selected candidates were evaluated, four graduated (active 10 → 14, +40%), and combined across the two growth sweeps **seven of eight evaluated candidates graduated — 87.5% yield**. A junk pool would have collapsed on the second batch; it held at 4/5. The candidate population is not exhausted, and selection budget — not candidate quality — remains the binding constraint on growth.

**The economic inversion.** The more consequential result is that **cost-per-graduation *improved* as K rose**, from $8.37 (K=3) to $7.01 (K=5) — the opposite of the diminishing returns most exploration systems exhibit. The cause is structural: under the evaluation quota each ladder runs the *full* rotation regardless of how many candidates are admitted, so harvested candidates ride along nearly for free (each extra slot is one more entry at the tail, ≈ $2). Ladder execution is a *fixed* cost; candidate evaluation is *marginal*. As long as candidates keep graduating, raising K amortizes the fixed full-rotation cost over more graduations and *improves* graduations-per-dollar. Candidate slots were never the scarce resource — running the ladder is.

**Two operating modes.** The two objectives are genuinely different and should not share a configuration. *Canonical mode* (K=3, quota=0, greedy order) optimizes for finding breaches cheaply (~$0.35 per breach, median winner rank 0) and remains the routine-reproduction default. *Growth mode* (K = quota, starvation order, K promoted 3→5 on this evidence) optimizes for repertoire growth (~$7 per graduation), run deliberately. We encode growth mode as a single wrapper that *locks quota = K*, eliminating a real mis-configuration (a K larger than the quota silently evaluates only `quota` of the K selected candidates) that nearly wasted a paid run.

**The stopping rule.** The remaining unknown is the *saturation point* — the largest K still worth paying for, beyond which the pool runs out of strong candidates and cost-per-graduation turns upward. The rule is metric-driven: raise K each growth sweep while cost-per-graduation stays flat or improves; stop when it rises sharply. A subsequent K=20 probe (§5.3b) sharpened this. Two things moved in opposite directions: graduation *yield* fell from the small-N 87.5% to 40% on the fresh least-tried tail — the pool's strong head is genuinely being exhausted — yet cost-per-graduation kept *falling*, to \$1.44, because the fixed full-rotation cost is amortized over more graduations (Figure F4). So the saturation point defined on yield arrives well before the one defined on dollars, and the dollar metric is the one that should govern the stopping rule: as long as cost-per-graduation keeps falling, a deeper sweep is buying permanent capability cheaply even as each individual candidate is less likely to pay off. On current evidence that point is still past K=20 and unmeasured.

## 6b. Finding 4 — Scheduling is capability: better strategy *order* converts missed goals into breaches

The findings above concern *which* strategies are admitted to the rotation and *how many*. This one concerns the *order* in which a fixed admitted set is executed — and it is the result we regard as the strongest single-variable demonstration in the work, because it shows that scheduling a fixed repertoire is not merely a cost optimization but a **capability lever**: re-ordering alone, with the repertoire, judge, corpus, and target all held fixed, *increases the fraction of goals that breach at all*.

**The controlled experiment.** We fix the entire escalation apparatus — the same ~28-strategy repertoire across the same five tiers, the same judge (v3), the same corpus of goals, the same target model — and change exactly one variable: the strategy *ordering mode* (`ROGUE_LADDER_ORDER`). Three modes are relevant. `fixed` applies the hand-coded order with no reorder. `canonical` (the production baseline) reorders *within* each tier by global Laplace-smoothed breach rate, keeps the planner tier pinned last, and is the routine-reproduction default of §6. `contextual` is the new mode: it sorts strategies *across* all five tiers into one execution order — so a terminal planner strategy can rise to the front — scored by a scope-widening blend `0.5·global + 0.3·vendor + 0.2·family + exploration` (vendor/family parsed from the target model). Because "reorder, never exclude" is preserved, every mode runs over the identical strategy set; only their sequence differs, so any outcome delta is attributable to ordering and nothing else. This is a clean single-variable design, not a confound.

**The headline: rank↓ *caused* ASR↑.** The naive expectation was "same winner, found earlier" — a pure latency/cost win at constant coverage. The measured effect is more interesting and more consequential: contextual ordering breached goals that the baseline order *left unbreached*. The mechanism is reachability under a budget. Each scan runs under a per-scan depth/budget cap; the old order (canonical/fixed) spent that cap on low-yield front tiers and, for some goals, the cap was exhausted *before the winning strategy ran*, so the goal was recorded as "held" (unbreached) when in fact a winner existed deeper in the ladder it never reached. Contextual ordering promotes that winner ahead of the cap, so the goal breaches. Better ordering therefore **converts previously-unreachable winners into realized breaches** — it does not merely find the same breach sooner. This is the same reachability-under-a-cap mechanism as Finding 2 (starved candidates), now operating on *strategy order* rather than *candidate admission*: in both cases the binding constraint is "the winner was never evaluated," and the fix is to make it reachable.

**Pilot (proof-of-concept).** `fixed` vs `contextual` on 20 AdvBench goals against Claude Haiku ($24.26), with contextual run *cold* (no vendor/family telemetry accumulated yet, so the blend reduces to global rate + exploration — this isolates the **cross-tier promotion** effect alone, with vendor-conditioning an unmeasured second-wave gain):

| metric | `fixed` (reorder off) | `contextual` (cold) | Δ |
|---|---|---|---|
| median winner-rank | 24 | 11 | **−54%** |
| best rank | 19 | 0 (breach at depth 1) | immediate breach now possible |
| ASR | 30% (6/20) | 45% (9/20) | **+50% relative** |
| cost / success | \$2.32 | \$1.15 | **−50%** |

**Option E (vs the production baseline).** Because `fixed` is reorder-*off* rather than the production `canonical` baseline, the pilot's rank delta overstates the production-relevant effect. Option E closes that gap: `canonical` (the production baseline) vs `contextual`, on 20 goals balanced across two datasets (10 AdvBench + 10 JBB), against Claude Haiku ($21.41).

| metric | `canonical` (production baseline) | `contextual` (cold) | Δ |
|---|---|---|---|
| median winner-rank — AdvBench | 22 | 13.5 | lower |
| median winner-rank — JBB | 22 | 11 | lower |
| ASR | 50% (10/20) | 60% (12/20) | **+20% relative** |
| cost / success | \$1.25 | \$0.74 | **−41%** |
| total cost | \$12.49 | \$8.92 | lower |
| best depth (AdvBench / JBB) | 19 / 16 | 1 / 1 | immediate breach now possible |

The direction is **consistent across both datasets independently** — the cross-tier promotion helps AdvBench and JBB separately, not as an aggregate artifact. Holding attacks, ladder, corpus, and judge fixed and changing only the order, **coverage (ASR), cost-per-success, and latency (rank/depth) all improved simultaneously** — there is no axis traded away, which is the signature of a capability gain rather than a cost re-allocation.

**Target-conditional optimality — why cross-tier matters and within-tier cannot.** The same fixed ladder is not equally well-ordered for every target. Run #0 (frozen, `canonical`, 30 goals/dataset) put Claude Haiku's median winner-rank at **17–18**, with the winners (crescendo / actor_attack plus cold harvested techniques) pinned to the *terminal planner tier*; the *identical* ladder breaks Mistral-Small at **rank 0** (`image:mml:wr` at the front). The optimal order is therefore **target-conditional**, and this is precisely why a *within-tier* reorder (the canonical baseline) structurally cannot capture it: the Claude-favourable winners live in a terminal tier that within-tier reordering never relocates. Only a *cross-tier* reorder can move a terminal-tier strategy to the front, which is why contextual — not a better within-tier heuristic — is what unlocks the missed breaches.

**Why this is publishable.** (1) It reframes red-team efficiency away from attack *generation* — the dominant axis in the literature — toward *scheduling* of a fixed repertoire. (2) Its primary KPIs are rank-of-winner and cost-per-breach (economic), not ASR in isolation. (3) Target-conditional optimal ordering (Mistral rank-0 vs Claude rank-17/18 on the identical ladder) is a clean, generalizable systems observation. (4) The ASR↑=capability causal finding — depth-cap reachability converting held goals into breaches — is the centerpiece. (5) Cross-tier vs within-tier is a structural argument, not a tuning one: a within-tier reorder *cannot* capture target-specific winners that live in a terminal tier. (6) The "reorder, never exclude" discipline keeps the experiment clean and reproducibility-preserving (the five prior modes stay byte-identical, so Run #0 reproducibility is intact). (7) Scaling: ordering is the first viable step toward a 10^6–10^7-primitive future — fingerprint the target, prioritize, run the top candidates — without changing ladder semantics.

**Honest limitations.** N=20 in each run, and the per-dataset medians are taken over breached *subsets of differing size* (e.g. 10 vs 12), so the rank medians are directional; the load-bearing evidence is the *paired* ASR and cost-per-success deltas together with the both-datasets consistency, not the medians. Contextual was run **cold** in both experiments, so the vendor/family conditioning never contributed — the measured effect is the cross-tier promotion alone, and the vendor claim remains unmeasured until at least two target families carry accumulated telemetry. A powered multi-target study (≥2 target families, ~100 goals/dataset, full 3-mode design) is ~\$400–700 and is parked in `docs/RESEARCH_TODO.md`; the pilot and Option E are the proof-of-concept that motivates it, not the powered paper themselves. Total measurement spend for the scheduling experiments to date is \$45.67.

## 7. The self-expansion loop

The final component decides *when* to pay for growth without a human in the loop. The **Growth Scheduler** is a deliberately deterministic rule over inventory already tracked — no bandit, no new telemetry: run growth mode when the candidate pool is large enough to justify the fixed full-rotation cost (`candidate_pool ≥ MIN_POOL`, default 5), otherwise stay canonical. It is self-regulating in the correct direction: a growth sweep graduates candidates, which drains the pool below the threshold, so the scheduler reverts to cheap canonical sweeps until harvesting refills the pool.

```
harvest ──▶ candidate pool grows ──▶ pool ≥ 5 ? ──▶ GROWTH sweep ──▶ candidates graduate
   ▲                                                                        │
   └──────── pool refills (more harvesting) ◀── CANONICAL (cheap) ◀── pool drains ◀─┘
```

This closes the loop the project set out to build — *harvest → schedule → grow → graduate → repeat* — so the system does not merely *demonstrate* that it can self-expand but, given a harvest cadence, actually *does*, as system behaviour rather than a manual decision. The dispatch path is opt-in (the scheduler reports its decision by default and only acts when explicitly told to), so automation is a deliberate operational choice rather than a default that silently spends.

## 8. Reproducibility and operations

We regard the operational substrate as part of the contribution, because adaptive-systems results that are not reproducible or that hide their failure modes are not results.

- **Determinism where it matters.** Grammar instantiation and renderer outputs are deterministic (e.g., SHA-256-seeded transforms rather than process-`hash()`), so the same input yields identical bytes across runs — a precondition for the breach matrix to be a stable artifact and for the *zero-cost quota simulation* below to be valid.
- **A zero-cost simulation pre-screened paid experiments.** Because the quota mechanic is deterministic given a logged rotation, we replayed the rotation-membership rows to bound reachability and cost under quota 0/1/2/3 *before* spending. It showed, decisively, that the cost of suppressing early-stop is *binary* at quota 0→1 ($2.80 → $18.45, ≈6×, since reaching even one starved candidate runs almost the whole ladder) while quota 1→3 adds nothing further, and that planner-tier reachability jumps from 0.07 toward full once the cap lifts — establishing the operational rule "if you pay the suppression cost at all, go to the full quota" for free.
- **Hardening learned from failure.** An early paid run hung for ~8 hours on a single un-timed-out provider request. We subsequently put a hard per-request timeout and bounded retries on every provider client (planner, target panel, judge) and a wall-clock watchdog around paid runs, and we use a process-watcher so completion is observed rather than polled.
- **Honest cost accounting.** One backbone is currently priced at $0 in the cost log, so planner spend is unaccounted and our cost-per-breach / cost-per-graduation figures are computed from target and judge calls; we flag this rather than silently absorb it.

## 9. Threats to validity and limitations

- **Statistical power.** The grammar-efficacy A/B was variance-dominated (arms swapped order across runs at N≈16/arm); single paid sweeps are directional, not significance-tested. The causal and K results rest on *categorical* changes (0 → 3 graduations; reachability 0.07 → 0.98; yield 87.5%) large relative to plausible noise, not on tight intervals.
- **Two batches, not a controlled K curve.** The K=3 and K=5 runs used *different* candidate batches (the first three graduated and left the pool), so the K result is "two growth sweeps both succeeded," not a controlled marginal-K curve on identical candidates; the 87.5% combined yield is the robust figure, and the saturation point is unmapped.
- **Within-tier vs cross-tier.** Starvation-aware *ordering* operates within tiers and breaks the image-tier monopoly, but the cross-tier reach into the candidate tier came from the *quota*, not the ordering; the two are complements, and conflating them would misattribute the effect.
- **Fixed primitive set and noisy EVADE classification.** Both sweeps used the same 40 primitives selected by reproducibility score, and the growth runs used a single baseline trial per cell, making EVADE-band classification noisier than the multi-trial baseline. Results may not extend to the full corpus.
- **Single deployment, evolving panel.** Findings are on one live system with a six-model panel; per-model numbers (e.g., the allocation-bias magnitudes) are panel- and period-specific.

## 10. Related work

The escalation tier draws its multi-turn strategy framing from Crescendo-style stepwise escalation (Russinovich et al., 2024) and the PyRIT tooling lineage, and its strategy *library* (not its agent) from ARMS; we deliberately took ARMS's strategy taxonomy but not its ε-greedy memory / RL agent, building instead a deterministic scheduler whose next increment would be the bandit ARMS implies. Our contribution is orthogonal to the large body of work on *attack effectiveness* (which technique breaks which model): we study the *orchestration system* that decides what to evaluate and how to allocate a fixed evaluation budget across a self-growing repertoire, and the telemetry that makes those decisions legible. The bandit literature (Thompson sampling, contextual and hierarchical bandits) is the natural future formalization of our deterministic scheduler; we argue (§11) for deferring it until the measured bottleneck — evaluation budget, not ordering — is mapped.

## 11. Discussion and future work

The recurring shape of every finding is identical: an intuition that growth is gated by technique supply or quality, a telemetry change that reveals the true gate is *orchestration*, and a small, deliberately-bounded change that repairs it. The project crossed two thresholds — first that allocation *affects* growth, then that allocation *budget* is one of its dominant drivers — which is a stronger and more actionable systems result than another scheduler heuristic.

**Immediate, paid, hypothesis-driven:** the *K=8 saturation probe* (§6), which the stopping rule turns into a decision procedure rather than open-ended exploration. A zero-cost projection (`scripts/simulate_k_cost.py`) puts a full-coverage K=8 growth sweep at ≈$32 with a shallow ~$0.93/K cost slope (candidates ride nearly free), so the probe is one cheap run; the irreducible unknown it answers is whether candidates 6–8 *graduate*. This is the single most paper-strengthening next step — it converts the headline K-result from two points to a measured curve. The prioritized research-action list (this item first, then contextual per-model routing; Thompson/hierarchical/3b-v2 explicitly deferred) lives in `docs/RESEARCH_TODO.md`.

**Adaptive Technique Prioritization (ATP) — cross-tier + vendor-conditioned scheduling.** The cross-tier ordering result is now a measured finding, reported in full as **§6b** above (pilot + Option E: ASR 30→45% and 50→60%, cost-per-success −50% / −41%, rank↓ on both datasets, with the rank↓⇒ASR↑ depth-cap mechanism as the centerpiece). Mechanically: the opt-in `ROGUE_LADDER_ORDER=contextual` mode (a) sorts strategies *across* all tiers into one execution order — so terminal planner strategies can rise to the front — and (b) scores each by a scope-widening blend `0.5·global + 0.3·vendor + 0.2·family` plus an additive exploration term, vendor/family parsed from the target model. It is a guarded path that leaves all five prior modes byte-identical, preserving Run #0 reproducibility. The two experiments to date ran contextual **cold** (no accumulated vendor/family telemetry), so the realized effect is the *cross-tier promotion* alone; the vendor/family conditioning accrues as freshly-tagged telemetry (migration 0025) lands, and validating it is the next paid measurement — a powered multi-target study (≥2 target families, ~100 goals/dataset, full 3-mode design, ~\$400–700, in `docs/RESEARCH_TODO.md`) that turns §6b's proof-of-concept into publication-grade numbers and measures the cold→warm vendor increment.

**Still deferred until saturation is mapped** (all answer *how to reorder/route*, while the live lever is *how big the budget is*; posterior machinery on a young, sparse substrate would overfit): **Thompson sampling** (give each strategy a Beta posterior over its breach rate and sample, making exploration/exploitation automatic — the principled replacement for our deterministic reorder and its manual exploration knobs); **per-model contextual maturation** (the ATP blend keys on target *vendor* and *family*, a coarse-but-immediately-usable proxy; a true per-`(strategy × model)` driver still needs *per-model ladders* rather than the short-circuiting multi-model panel); **hierarchical priors** (partial pooling so a thin `family × model × strategy` cell borrows strength from its fatter `family`-level parent, buying model-specificity without N samples per cell); and **persistent arm-state**.

**Renderer self-synthesis (3b-v2).** The candidate self-growth loop graduates *textual* techniques. Its renderer-tier analogue would have an LLM *write a new image/audio renderer's code*, a sandbox *execute and verify* it (safe? deterministic?), and a human approve before it goes active — ROGUE inventing new *executable* capabilities, not just harvesting textual ones. It is deferred specifically because executing model-generated code is a real safety surface; the structural invariant that a synthesized renderer can never reach active without sandbox → determinism → approval was built first, precisely so this can be added safely later.

---

## 12. Conclusion

We set out to test a single chain — *reachable → tested → breach → graduate* — in a self-growing LLM red-team, and found that a cost-optimal scheduler had silently broken its first link while every conventional log showed the system working. The right telemetry made the break measurable; a permissive planner and deterministic grammars removed an upstream provider gate; a bounded allocation change converted invisible candidates into graduated capabilities; and the evaluation budget turned out to be a cheap, dominant growth lever, automated by a deterministic scheduler that closes the self-expansion loop. The durable lesson is methodological: in an adaptive system, *measure opportunity, not only outcome* — the instrumentation that records what could have happened but did not is what surfaces the real constraints, and what keeps you from optimizing prematurely against the ones you already understand.
